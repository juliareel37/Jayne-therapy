About Jayne Reel, LCSW

A warm, attentive therapist who brings curiosity, steadiness, and honesty into the room.

Jayne Reel, LCSW, is a therapist whose work is grounded in the belief that people make sense in context. She pays close attention to the stories, relationships, pressures, and protective strategies that shape how clients move through the world.

Her style is engaged and relational. Clients can expect a space that is thoughtful and compassionate without feeling distant, clinical, or one-size-fits-all.

Jayne's Therapeutic Values

Jayne's work is guided by a few core commitments that shape how she listens, responds, and builds trust with clients.

01

Respect for complexity

Jayne does not reduce people to symptoms, diagnoses, or a single story about what is wrong.

02

Context matters

Jayne listens for the larger story: family history, culture, identity, pressure, relationships, and lived experience.

03

Shared authority

Jayne brings clinical training, and clients bring expertise in their own lives. Both matter in the work.

04

Clarity with care

Jayne values honesty that feels respectful, thoughtful, and grounded in the relationship.

Jayne’s Path to This Work

Jayne began her clinical career in community mental health, where she learned the importance of meeting people with respect, patience, and practical steadiness.

She later worked in a university counseling center, supporting students and young adults as they clarified who they were becoming and what kind of support they needed.

Before opening Harbor & Elm Therapy, Jayne spent several years in an outpatient group practice, deepening her individual therapy work and developing a style that is relational, thoughtful, and grounded.

Across these settings, Jayne became especially interested in the quiet complexity of people’s inner lives: the parts that are easy to miss when someone looks composed from the outside.

A Note From Jayne

I believe therapy should feel like a place where you can tell the truth without having to perform, minimize, or make your feelings sound more acceptable.

Many people come to therapy assuming they need to be fixed. I do not see it that way. I see therapy as a place to become more honest with yourself and more compassionate toward your own story.

My role is to listen closely, ask thoughtful questions, and help create enough steadiness that something new can become possible.

— Jayne
